An index at record highs can hide a market quietly hollowing out.

Every chart below is queried live from World Bank market data, right in your browser. Scroll the argument, then put your own questions to fifty years of stockmarket history across six countries.

Chapter 01 — The vanishing
—

Half the market walked away.

America's count of listed companies peaked in 1996 and has fallen ever since. Mergers ate some. Private equity took others. And thousands of firms that once would have gone public simply never came.

Chapter 02 — The survivors
—

The ones that stayed got giant.

While the market lost half its names, its value kept climbing against the whole economy. Fewer, bigger companies carry more weight than the crowded market of the 1990s ever did. That is the giga in giga-IPO.

Chapter 03 — The twist
—

It isn't happening everywhere.

This is a Western condition, not a global one. China and India now list thousands more companies than America, and even Japan edges it. The public company isn't dying. It's moving.
